Herd Immunity — Community Armor

When enough people are immune, outbreaks hit a wall.

Estimated read time: ~2 min

Herd immunity is the network effect of immunity. If a virus hops into a community where most people are vaccinated or recovered, each jump fails and the chain dies out. The required coverage isn’t arbitrary: it depends on how contagious the virus is. Measles — a social butterfly — needs ~95% coverage. Pertussis, rubella, and others have lower but still demanding thresholds.

Coverage is local, not national. One undervaccinated school or neighborhood can ignite an outbreak even if the national average looks fine. The first to benefit from solid coverage are people who can’t be protected directly: newborns, transplant patients, people on chemo, or anyone whose immune system struggles.

Why It Matters

Your shot isn’t just for you. It’s a brick in a wall that shields the people with the least defense.

Closing Line

Strong herd immunity is quiet — you only notice it when it’s gone.
